Charles Perrault (1628–1703) pioneered the literary fairy tale with his 1697 collection, *Histoires ou contes du temps passé*. He transformed oral folk traditions into sophisticated narratives for the French court, producing classics like "Cinderella," "Sleeping Beauty," and "Bluebeard." These stories introduced the "once upon a time" convention and featured explicit *moralités*. By elevating peasant folklore to high literature, Perrault laid the foundation for the modern genre, influencing the Brothers Grimm and securing a permanent place in global culture.
