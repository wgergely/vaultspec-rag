Charles Perrault (1628–1703) was a French author who formalized the fairy tale genre. His 1697 collection, *Histoires ou contes du temps passé* (Tales of Mother Goose), adapted folk oral traditions into literary works. Notable tales include "Cinderella," "Sleeping Beauty," and "Little Red Riding Hood." Perrault introduced narrative conventions like "once upon a time" and appended moral lessons to his stories. While now seen as children’s literature, his original versions often contained dark, cautionary themes. His work significantly influenced the Brothers Grimm and remains foundational to Western folklore and modern adaptations.
